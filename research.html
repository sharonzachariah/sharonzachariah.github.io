Arbor Green - Research

Due to massive cultural and language barriers between the military and civilian worlds, young soldiers of the ranks E4 and E5 find it challenging to transition into the right civilian career after separating from the military.

We used the following methods to better understand our users, while also considering other stakeholders such as their families, company recruiters, the Department of Defense, etc.

01

METHOD #1

We interviewed 19 different users to hear their side of the story.

We spoke with 8 recently transitioned E4s and E5s whom we met at veteran job fairs, through connections at veteran initiatives, and at a conference in Texas, 4 recruiters from major companies in the Pittsburgh area, and 7 subject matter experts, ranging from leaders of veteran initiatives to military social workers to data scientists.

02

METHOD #2

We conducted qualitative research with people from the Transitioning Veterans Program Office.

We had the opportunity to visit the Veteran Affairs Headquarters in Washington DC to sit in on a review of the Transition Assistance Program that soldiers are required to go through when they decide to separate.

03

METHOD #3

We did a competitive analysis of existing skills translators and their pros and cons.

We spent time on online military resources taking quizzes and tests that are aimed at helping a soldier figure out their next career step. Tools such as  MyNextMove, MOS Crosswalk, CareerScope and various other resources on O*NET were great independent sources of information.

04

METHOD #4

We conducted 40 hours of analysis and created maps and diagrams to understand our problem space.

Key Insights

Skills translators read the soldier's MOS (Military Occupational Specialty) code and suggest related careers. However, soldiers tend to have a lot of duties apart from what their MOS code defines.

INSIGHT 1

 

 

Trained career counselors can provide personalized attention to the soldier's background and experience, but around 200,000 soldiers transition every year, and there aren't enough counselors to help them.

INSIGHT 2

 

 

 

 

Young soldiers need help navigating the civilian workplace. It is not enough to tell them to become a lawyer; they need clear actions steps as to how they can get there.

INSIGHT 3

 

 

 

The lack of translation goes both ways. Company recruiters aren't able to value a soldier's experience because they don't understand most of it and soldiers have a hard time explaining their past duties in terms that are relevant to the recruiter.
